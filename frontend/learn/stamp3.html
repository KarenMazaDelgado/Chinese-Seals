“Zhao Clan, [Courtesy Name] Zi’ang”

Trace This Seal
Owner Zhao Mengfu (赵孟頫, 1254–1322)
Dynasty Yuan
Type Personal authentication seal
Stamped Year 1295, shortly after the painting’s completion
Current Location National Palace Museum, Taipei, China

Historical Background: Identity and Origin of the Seal

The seal reading “Zhao Shi Zi’ang” (赵氏子昂) — literally “Zhao clan, [courtesy name] Zi’ang”—is a personal authentication seal belonging to Zhao Mengfu (b.1254–d.1322). Zhao Mengfu, courtesy name Zi’ang, was a distinguished Chinese calligrapher, painter, and poet of the late Southern Song and early Yuan dynasty. He was the eleventh-generation descendant of Emperor Taizu of the Song dynasty, and a direct heir of Prince Qin, Zhao Defang.

The seal format—combining the clan name “Zhao” with the courtesy name “Zi’ang”—follows the classical surname-plus-courtesy-name convention popular among Song and Yuan literati. Autumn Colors on the Qiao and Hua Mountains was painted in the first year of the Yuanzhen era (1295), after Zhao Mengfu returned south from his post in Jinan, as a gift for his close friend Zhou Mi, depicting the landscape of Zhou’s ancestral homeland. This seal is stamped directly on the scroll and constitutes the most immediate physical marker of authorship on the work.

Among the many seals documented on this scroll, this seal appears as the very first one in the sequence; it is the earliest impression on the entire painting, with all subsequent seals belonging to later collectors and connoisseurs.

Artistic and Cultural Significance

The Seal as Calligraphy

Zhao Mengfu was not only a supreme painter and calligrapher but also an accomplished seal carver. He was conversant in poetry, literature, and musical theory, and his achievements in calligraphy and painting were of the highest order—he was celebrated as the “crown of Yuan dynasty art.” The seal’s archaic seal-script (zhuanshu) lettering mirrors the pursuit of antiquity (guyi) aesthetic he championed throughout his life, making the seal itself an artistic statement consistent with the scroll’s visual language.

A Living Embodiment of the “Painting and Calligraphy Share One Source”

Zhao Mengfu’s unparalleled mastery of calligraphy profoundly shaped his approach to painting. It was he who pioneered the practice of infusing brushwork from calligraphy into painting, establishing it as an enduring trend, and who advocated the principle that calligraphy and painting share the same origin. The “Zhao Shi Zi’ang” seal, together with Zhao’s own handwritten inscription on the scroll, forms a complete expression of this philosophy: the inscription is calligraphy, the landscape is painting, and the seal is seal-script—all three unified within a single artwork. This makes the scroll a canonical example of the literati ideal of integrating poetry, calligraphy, painting, and seal art (shi, shu, hua, yin) into one cohesive whole.

The Seal as the Anchor of Authenticity

In terms of seals and colophons, Autumn Colors on the Qiao and Hua Mountains is perhaps the most richly adorned scroll in the history of Chinese painting. Yet amid this accumulation of later hands, “Zhao Shi Zi’ang” stands alone as the original impression, functioning as the foundational evidence for attributing the work to Zhao Mengfu’s own brush—an indispensable anchor for art historical authentication.

Political and Identity Significance

A Declaration of Song Imperial Lineage

The word “Zhao” (赵) in the seal was far from an ordinary surname marker in the Yuan context. Zhao Mengfu was the eleventh-generation descendant of Emperor Taizu of Song, Zhao Kuangyin. Having entered service under the Yuan, he rose through a succession of offices including Academician of the Jixian Academy and Academician Reader-in-Waiting of the Hanlin Academy, ultimately reaching the rank of Academician承旨(Grand Academician) and Ronglu Dafu. By placing “Zhao Shi” at the head of his seal—stamped on every painting he authenticated—he was making a quiet but persistent claim, under Mongol rule, to his identity as a descendant of the Han Chinese Song imperial house.

A Literatus Caught Between Two Loyalties

Zhao Mengfu had been a surviving subject of the Southern Song who chose to serve the Yuan—becoming a high official of the conquering dynasty. He lived a life of profound inner contradiction, marked by both glory and moral ambiguity, earning the label “the most awkward man of his era.” In this light, the seal’s insistence on “Zhao Shi” reads as an act of self-reconciliation: a way of asserting ancestral pride and cultural continuity even while accepting office under foreign rulers. The small red impression encodes the spiritual tension felt by an entire generation of Han literati caught between pragmatic collaboration and the demands of loyalty.

The Gift to Zhou Mi

Zhou Mi was a celebrated connoisseur and literary figure of the late Song and early Yuan who, as a devoted Song loyalist, lived in voluntary retirement and refused to serve the Yuan dynasty. That Zhao Mengfu chose to present this painting—sealed with his own dynastic surname—to a man who represented the very ideal of Song loyalty he himself had abandoned, is deeply charged. The seal becomes a silent overture across a political divide: an acknowledgment of shared cultural roots, and perhaps an unspoken apology, between two men who had responded differently to the same historical catastrophe.
