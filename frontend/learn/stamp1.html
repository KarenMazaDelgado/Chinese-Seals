Historical Background: The Seal’s Origin

The seal reading “古希天子” (Gu Xi Tianzi)—a deliberate play on the classical phrase gu xi (古稀), meaning “rare to reach seventy”—belongs to the Qianlong Emperor. It was created to mark the singular occasion of his seventieth birthday in 1780.

The physical seal itself is a remarkable object in its own right. According to the emperor’s own inscription recorded alongside it, the “Gu Xi Tianzi” circular seal was carved from an antique jade axle-head: the original jade piece was cut in two, one half fashioned into an archer’s thumb ring, the other shaped into the round seal. Qianlong noted that the seal was carved in the autumn of the 47th year of his reign (1782) and used specifically to authenticate calligraphy and paintings. The fact that it was fashioned from a repurposed antique object was entirely deliberate: in Qianlong’s aesthetics, ancient jade carried accumulated cultural authority, and using it for his most personal commemorative seal was itself a statement about the depth of his connection to Chinese antiquity.

The Literary and Intellectual Origin of the Phrase

The phrase gu xi derives from a celebrated line by the Tang dynasty poet Du Fu: “It has been rare since antiquity for a man to live to seventy.” By the time Qianlong turned seventy, this saying had long been enshrined in Chinese literary consciousness as the classical benchmark of exceptional longevity. To celebrate his seventieth birthday in 1780, the Qianlong emperor composed an essay entitled “On Being Seventy” (Gu Xi Shuo). In it, he surveyed Chinese emperors who had lived into their seventies across the full span of history, argued that his own accomplishments comprehensively surpassed theirs, and explicitly declared himself the only truly wise and capable ruler among the group. The essay was not a meditation on aging but a triumphalist proclamation. The seal “Gu Xi Tianzi” is the compressed emblem of that proclamation: every time it was stamped on a masterwork of Chinese art, it reiterated the claim that the emperor reviewing it was not merely a patron or collector, but a figure of world-historical uniqueness.

Cultural and Artistic Significance

The Seal as a Biographical Timestamp

Qianlong’s seal usage was systematically age-coded: before the age of seventy, he used name seals; from seventy onward, he deployed “Gu Xi Tianzi” and its variants; from eighty onward, he used “八征耄念之宝(Treasure of the Eighty-Year-Old Man Mindful of the Eighth Principle)” and “自强不息Never Cease to Improve”; and after his abdication in 1795 he used “太上皇帝(Emperor Emeritus/Retired).” The appearance of “Gu Xi Tianzi” on Autumn Colors therefore precisely dates that particular act of engagement with the scroll to the period between 1780 and roughly 1790, when the next generation of age-seals took over. It functions as a biographical timestamp embedded directly into the surface of the painting.

A Culminating Act of Literati Connoisseurship

The most important blank spaces on Autumn Colors are filled with Qianlong’s imperial seals and colophons—nine colophons in all—to the point where almost no empty ground remains on either the painting surface or the adjoining sections. Among these, “Gu Xi Tianzi” occupies the position of supreme personal attestation. Where his other seals announce institutional ownership (“乾隆御览之宝,” “石渠宝笈”) or connoisseurial grading (“神品”), “Gu Xi Tianzi” is the most intimate: it is a self-portrait compressed into four characters, marking the point at which the emperor's most exalted conception of himself met the painting he most admired.

The Scroll’s Special Emotional Resonance for Qianlong

Qianlong’s relationship with Autumn Colorswas unusually personal. In 1748, while on an eastern tour to Shandong, he actually stood before the real Qiao and Hua mountains—then had the painting rushed from the imperial treasury by mounted courier so he could compare the living landscape with Zhao Mengfu’s seven-century-old depiction. He found the two mutually magnificent, and in the process corrected a geographical error in Zhao’s inscription, noting that the artist had misidentified which mountain lay to the east. This episode—a Manchu emperor physically verifying a Han Chinese masterwork against reality, then correcting the revered master—encapsulates the complex dynamic encoded in the seal: deep admiration coupled with imperial authority, intellectual engagement coupled with sovereign correction.

The Controversy of Saturation

The scroll carries a total of fifty-seven seals on the main painting surface alone, with sixteen more on the front interleaf and ninety-five on the rear—dominated above all by Qianlong’s own marks. For aesthetically oriented critics then and now, this density of imperial stamping, with “Gu Xi Tianzi” among the most physically prominent, represents a kind of sovereign overwriting of the original work. Zhao Mengfu’s spare, classicizing composition, which was specifically designed to evoke the restrained antiquity of the Tang and Song masters, finds itself bearing the jubilant self-coronation of an eighteenth-century Manchu emperor as its most visible overlay.

Dynastic Legitimacy Through Cultural Mastery

The Seal as a Biographical Timestamp

The Qianlong Emperor’s seal program on Chinese masterworks was at its core an act of political legitimation. The Qing dynasty was founded by Chinese Manchu minority, and throughout the eighteenth century its emperors invested enormous effort in demonstrating their right to the Mandate of Heaven by mastering the full range of Han Chinese cultural achievement. Qianlong, described as China’s longest-lived emperor, cultivated the sobriquets “Ancient Rare Son of Heaven” and “The Perfect Man of Ten Military Victories,” and presided over a court collection that at its peak included over ten thousand items, among them two thousand works of calligraphy and painting from the Jin, Tang, Song, and Yuan dynasties. Stamping “Gu Xi Tianzi” on Autumn Colors> was a declaration that the Manchu dynasty had legitimate reign in China as well as absorbed and surpassed the cultural heritage from its previous dynasties.

The Seal as a Claim of Historical Singularity

The specific wording of the seal is politically precise. By styling himself “Gu Xi Tianzi”, Qianlong was asserting that Chinese imperial history, in all its millennia of dynasties and sovereigns, had culminated in him. The phrase claims that no previous emperor had combined longevity, power, military success, cultural refinement, and moral authority to the same degree.

The Abdication Paradox

The seal also carries a subtle political irony. Qianlong had vowed early in his reign never to surpass the sixty-year reign of his grandfather, the Kangxi Emperor, whom he revered as the dynasty’s greatest ruler. When he did abdicate in 1795, he became Emperor Emeritus while continuing to exercise real power until his death in 1799. The “Gu Xi Tianzi” seal thus sits in retrospect as a monument to a career that was both spectacularly long and, in its final chapter, constitutionally evasive.
