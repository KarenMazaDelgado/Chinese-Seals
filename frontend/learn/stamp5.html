Historical Background

The seal reading “石渠宝笈” (Shiqu Baoji), translated variously as “Precious Works in Stone Canal Pavilion,” or “The Shiqu Catalogue of Imperial Painting and Calligraphy”, does not belong to an individual person but to an institution: the most ambitious imperial art catalogue ever compiled in Chinese history. It signifies that the work bearing it has been formally registered in that catalogue and therefore received into the highest tier of the Qing imperial collection. The name derives from the Shiqu Pavilion (Shiqu Ge) in the Han dynasty imperial palace, itself a reference to the legendary repositories of classical learning, invoking an ancient tradition of imperial stewardship over civilization’s cultural inheritance. The Qianlong and Jiaqing courts specifically appointed civil officials to compile painting and calligraphy catalogues documenting the Qing court’s collection, of which the Shiqu Baoji series was the most comprehensive and prestigious. The catalogue appeared in three successive compilations: the first Shiqu Baoji, completed in 1745 under the direction of Zhang Zhao and other high officials; the Xubian (continuation), completed in 1793; and the Sanbian (third series), completed in 1816 under the Jiaqing Emperor. Together, these three volumes constitute the most exhaustive inventory of imperial Chinese art ever assembled, cataloguing thousands of works of painting and calligraphy across all dynasties—their titles, dimensions, inscriptions, seals, colophons, and storage locations within the Forbidden City—all accompanied by the emperor’s own appreciations, comments, and poems.
